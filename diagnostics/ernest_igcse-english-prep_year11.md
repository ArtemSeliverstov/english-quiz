# Ernest — Cambridge IGCSE English: Year 11 Readiness & Prep

**Situation:** transferring into **Year 11** (final IGCSE year) at a Cambridge-board school in Almaty (likely Haileybury Almaty — Charterhouse has no Year 11 until later). This pack builds the skills a Year 11 English exam actually tests — the grammar work (see [practice set](ernest_practice-set_2026-07-07.md)) is the **floor**, not the whole exam.

**Which exam: confirmed IGCSE English as a Second Language (0510)** — the right fit for a B1/B2 EAL learner. Its papers are **Reading & Writing**, **Listening**, and **Speaking** (separately endorsed). Aim for the **Extended tier** (grades A*–E) rather than Core (C–G) — a capable Year 11 transfer should target the higher paper. Items marked **⭐** below are *not* on 0510 (they belong to First Language 0500) — treat them as optional enrichment only, skip if time is short.

## The five skills beyond grammar

Cambridge English isn't tested by MCQ grammar — it's these five. Each section below = what's tested + a real task Ernest can sit + a self-check.

### 1. Reading comprehension — find it, then infer it
Cambridge asks for explicit facts **and** meaning/attitude that isn't stated directly, answered **in his own words** (copying whole sentences loses marks).

**Read this, then answer:**
> *Every year, thousands of teenagers move to a new country and start again at an unfamiliar school. Researchers who study these students have found that the first term is usually the hardest. Newcomers often feel confident about their subjects but struggle with the small things: local slang, unwritten social rules, and the speed of everyday conversation. Students who join clubs or sports teams in their first month settle in far more quickly than those who wait. According to one study, it is not academic ability but a willingness to make mistakes out loud that predicts how fast a newcomer makes friends. Teachers add that the students who ask the most questions — even simple ones — are rarely the weakest; they are usually the ones who adapt fastest. Within a year, most transfer students report that the move, though frightening at first, became the experience that made them more independent than peers who never left home.*

1. Which part of the year do newcomers find hardest?
2. Give **two** "small things" newcomers struggle with.
3. In your own words, what does *"a willingness to make mistakes out loud"* mean?
4. Why do teachers say the students who ask the most questions are "rarely the weakest"? *(inference)*
5. Overall, is the writer's view of moving schools positive or negative? Explain in your own words, using one idea from the text. *(0510 tests this — reading for attitude.)*

*Self-check: answers 1–2 are in the text; 3–5 need his own words. If he copied a full sentence, redo it.*

### 2. Summary writing — the highest-value IGCSE skill
Condense a passage to its key points, **in his own words**, **within a word limit**. The two classic losses: copying phrases, and going over the limit.

**Task:** *In about 50 words, summarise what helps a new student settle in quickly.* (Points to find: join clubs/sports early · be willing to speak up and make mistakes · ask questions.)
*Self-check: exactly the key points, no examples, own words, ≤ 55 words.*

### 3. Directed writing — email / report / article
Write for a **purpose, audience, and format**. Marks split between *task coverage* and *language*.

**Task:** *Write an email (120–150 words) to a friend in Bahrain about your first month at the new school. Cover: one thing that surprised you, one difficulty, one good thing.*
*Rubric — score each /3: **Format** (greeting + sign-off + email tone) · **Task** (all three points, developed) · **Range** (varied vocabulary/linkers) · **Accuracy** (a/the, tense, prepositions).*

### 4. Descriptive / narrative writing
Cambridge rewards **engaging the reader**: precise vocabulary, the five senses, controlled structure — not just correct sentences.

**Task:** *Describe your first morning arriving at a school where you know no one. Focus on what you see, hear and feel. (150–200 words.)*
*Checklist: show-don't-tell (not "I was nervous" → show it) · at least three senses · varied sentence openings · consistent tense.*

### 5. ⭐ Language analysis (First Language 0500 / Literature)
"How does the writer create an effect?" — pick words/phrases and explain their impact.

**Task:** In sentence *"the move, though frightening at first, became the experience that made them more independent,"* how does the writer show the change from fear to growth? Comment on **two** words.

### 6. Speaking / interview
Haileybury interviews; some Cambridge routes assess speaking. Practise **talking for a minute** on:
- "Moving to a new country is the best thing that can happen to a teenager — do you agree?"
- "Describe a time you had to adapt to something new."
- "What makes a good friend when you're the new person?"

## Simple mark bands (for tasks 2–5)

| Band | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| **Strong** | All task points, own words, varied vocabulary, few errors, reads naturally |
| **Secure** | Points covered, some range, minor errors that don't block meaning |
| **Developing** | Points thin or lifted, basic vocabulary, errors slow the reader |

Target for a confident Year 11 transfer: **Secure → Strong**.

## Weekly plan (integrates the grammar set)

| Day | Focus |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | Grammar practice set + Reading task (§1) |
| 3 | Summary (§2) — do it twice, hit the word limit |
| 4 | Directed writing email (§3) + 3-pass proofread |
| 5 | Descriptive writing (§4) + 3-pass proofread |
| 6 | Speaking prompts (§6) aloud; ⭐ language analysis (§5) if on 0500 |
| Ongoing | Every written piece ends with the **three proofread passes** (a/the · tense & have+V3 · small word after the verb) |

**Bottom line:** the grammar leaks are nearly closed; these five skills are what a Year 11 Cambridge examiner is actually marking. Work through one section per sitting and we'll have a real read on where he stands against the level — not just his grammar accuracy.
