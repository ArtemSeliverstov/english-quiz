2. The Three-Layer Problem

Articles are harder than PVs to remediate because Russian has no article system at all — there is no L1 transfer, only L1 absence. PV learners have some L1 anchor (Russian prefix verbs map loosely to English particles). Article learners have nothing. This creates three distinct failure layers:

Layer 1 — Rule knowledge gap (B1 rules not fully internalised)

Some B1 rules are still shaky: countable vs uncountable, zero article with abstract nouns, zero article with time expressions. Evidence: Ernest picks "a good progress" (uncountable), Artem writes "the next Monday" (time expression). These are declarative knowledge failures — the rule isn't stored.

Layer 2 — Decision-making under ambiguity (the a/the swap)

This is Artem's primary failure mode. He knows an article is needed but defaults to "a" when "the" is required. The "Do we both know which one?" heuristic showed immediate improvement in exercise sessions — but it hasn't automated yet. The pattern: every time a noun has been contextually established (second mention, shared knowledge, unique referent), Artem reaches for "a" instead of "the". This accounts for ~40% of his article errors in exercises.

Layer 3 — Fixed expression blind spots

English has hundreds of fixed expressions where article choice is lexically determined, not rule-governed: "in the saddle" (not "a"), "take action" (not "the action"), "Board of Directors" (no "the"), "at the stage" vs "at a stage". These can't be derived from rules — they must be memorised as chunks. Current quiz coverage of these patterns is limited to ~40 aph-series questions.

3. Error Taxonomy — The 5 Failure Patterns

Across 9 exercise sessions (Artem) + quiz data for all players, article errors cluster into 5 distinct patterns. Each requires a different intervention.

Pattern 1 a → the swap (shared-knowledge nouns) ~40% of errors

Recognises article is needed, but defaults to "a" when context demands "the". Root cause: Russian has no definiteness marker — the speaker/writer must learn to track discourse reference, which is an entirely new cognitive operation.

a view→the view (unique referent — only one view from that spot)
a support van→the support van (shared knowledge — both speaker and listener know which one)
a hotel manager→the hotel manager (situational uniqueness — one manager at that hotel)
an outcome→the outcome (inherited specificity — "the outcome of the study")
a meal→the meal (second mention / previously established)

Key heuristic: "Do we both know which one?" → if yes, use THE. Showed immediate improvement in exercises but hasn't automated.

Pattern 2 Phantom articles (adding article where ∅ is correct) ~25% of errors

Inserts an article before uncountable/abstract nouns or in fixed time expressions. Root cause: once the learner starts "feeling" that articles matter, they overcorrect and insert them everywhere.

a progress→∅ progress (uncountable noun)
a Python code→∅ Python code (uncountable — Ernest)
the next Monday→∅ next Monday (next/last + time = zero article)
a Wednesday→∅ Wednesday (day of week = zero article)
the management→∅ management (abstract concept in general sense)
Pattern 3 Preposition+article patterns (the N of…, in ∅ N) ~15% of errors

Confused by the interaction between prepositions and articles. "The X of Y" usually takes THE (the feasibility of the project), but "in + abstract noun" often takes ∅ (in procurement, in progress). These patterns don't exist in Russian.

missed the feasibility of → tried removing "the" before abstract noun
missed in ∅ procurement → added "the" before abstract noun after preposition
missed the preparation of → inconsistent with "the X of" pattern
most of riders→most of the riders
Pattern 4 Fixed expression errors ~12% of errors

Article choice is lexically frozen — no rule derivation possible. Must be memorised as chunks.

in a saddle→in the saddle (fixed expression)
board's meeting→board meeting (compound noun — no article, no possessive)
take the action→take action (fixed phrase — zero article)
the Board of the Directors→the Board of Directors (proper noun pattern)
Pattern 5 Dropped articles (omission) ~8% of errors

Simply forgetting to include any article at all. Least frequent for Artem (he's past the omission stage) but still significant for Nicole and Ernest.

dropped the before "management team" and "performance"
it's tough deadline→it's a tough deadline
missing a before "new IT tool"

6. The Holistic Plan (3 Phases)

Phase 1: Fix Existing Problems (Week 1) Immediate

A. Question quality fixes — review and fix the 10 cross-player failure questions (art14, art15, art11, art_b08, art_c03, art01, mc12, mc14, etc.). Issues: generic "Which is correct?" MCQ stems, unclear fixed-expression rules in exp, multi-blank difficulty compounding.

B. Exp quality audit — verify all article exp fields have contrastive structure (Rule → Why → ✗ wrong form → L1 note). Many aph/art-series exp fields were flagged in the S49 no-contrast audit.

C. Add 7 gap-scaffold questions targeting the a→the swap (Pattern 1) — similar to PV phase 1 scaffold questions (pv_s01–s07). Focus: shared-knowledge nouns in business and cycling contexts. Each question's exp should reinforce the "Do we both know which one?" heuristic.

D. Design rule: two-sentence discourse stems — research shows 32–70% of article decisions require cross-sentence context (Brown 1983, Jonz 1990). All new B2+ article gap/input questions must use two-sentence stems by default: Sentence 1 establishes the referent; Sentence 2 contains the blank. Isolated single-sentence stems are acceptable only for zero-article rules and fixed expressions.

Effort~15 question fixes + 7 new questions
Phase 2: Targeted Expansion by Error Pattern (Weeks 2–4) Core work

Pattern 1 — a→the decision drills (20 questions)

Gap + input questions built around discourse-tracking: two-sentence stems where the first sentence introduces a noun with "a" and the second requires "the". Business memos, cycling narratives, school stories. Force the learner to track reference across sentences.

Pattern 2 — Zero-article decision drills (15 questions)

Time expressions (next/last + time), uncountable nouns in business context (progress, information, advice, equipment, research), abstract nouns used generically (management, procurement, performance). Gap questions where ∅ competes with "the" or "a".

Pattern 3 — Preposition+article patterns (12 questions)

Expand art_c series: "the X of Y" vs "in ∅ X" contrast pairs. E.g., "the feasibility of the project" but "in procurement". Include "most of the X" pattern. Business register essential.

Pattern 4 — Business fixed expressions (10 questions)

Compound nouns (∅ board meeting, ∅ risk management, ∅ performance review), institutional phrases (take ∅ action, make ∅ progress, reach ∅ agreement), proper noun patterns (the Board of Directors, the CFO). Mostly input type — these must be memorised.

B1 foundation boost for Nicole/Ernest (10 questions)

Basic rules still shaky at B1: a/an with professions, institutions (go to ∅ school/hospital), sports/meals (play ∅ football, have ∅ breakfast). Nicole themes: K-pop, school. Ernest themes: GCSE, basketball, gym.

NEW — Fluctuation Hypothesis diagnostics (8 questions)

Ionin et al. (2004) showed Russian speakers' errors concentrate in two cells of a [±definite × ±specific] matrix. 8 questions (2 per cell) using prefix art_dx to diagnose whether errors reflect specificity-based reasoning. The two "mismatch" cells ([+def,−spec]: "the manager on duty, whoever that is" and [−def,+spec]: "a particular report I had in mind") are the hardest. If a player gets those wrong but the convergence cells right, the exp names the specific confusion. See Section 9 for theoretical basis.

NEW — NP complexity variation rule

REALEC corpus data shows article omission spikes when adjectives precede the noun ("important role" → omits "an"). For all new article questions, systematically vary NP complexity: ~50% bare NP (___ report), ~30% modified NP (___ quarterly report), ~20% heavily modified NP (___ recently completed quarterly report). This tests processing automaticity, not just rule knowledge.

Effort~67 new questions across 5 batches

B. C1 precision (10 questions) — generic THE (the telephone, the guitar), article with acronyms/abbreviations (the UN, ∅ NATO), article in formal registers (the aforementioned, the former). Egor and Artem focus.

8. Exercise Session Changes

New Exercise Type: Article Decision Drill

A dedicated exercise format for article practice, designed around the 5 error patterns:

Format: Claude presents a 2–3 sentence mini-scenario with article blanks. The player fills in a/an/the/∅ for each blank. After each item, Claude provides immediate corrective feedback linking to the specific error pattern.

Scaffolding sequence (based on Master's binary system — see Section 9):

LevelFocusDecision frameworkExample prompt style
Stage 1Binary: classify or identify?Master's core question: "Are you saying what kind (a/∅) or which one (the)?""We hired a consultant. ___ consultant recommended restructuring."
Stage 2Ternary: THE, A, or ∅?Add zero article: uncountable, abstract, time expressions"___ management needs to make ___ progress on ___ project."

Key design rule: start each drill with "convergence" items ([+def,+spec] and [−def,−spec]) to build confidence, then introduce "mismatch" items. Feedback must name the specific error pattern, not just state the correct answer.

9. Research Basis & Design Principles

The following research findings directly inform the quiz and exercise design in this plan. References are abbreviated — the full research report is in the project file.

The Fluctuation Hypothesis (Ionin, Ko & Wexler 2004)

Learners from article-less L1s (Russian, Chinese, Korean) have UG access to two universal parameters — definiteness (English system) and specificity (whether the speaker has a particular referent in mind) — and fluctuate between them. This produces errors concentrated in two "mismatch cells" of a 2×2 matrix:

ContextTargetRussian speaker errorWhy
[+definite, +specific]the✓ Usually correctBoth parameters converge
[+definite, −specific]theOveruses "a"Non-specific → perceived as indefinite
[−definite, +specific]aOveruses "the"Specific → perceived as definite
[−definite, −specific]a✓ Usually correctBoth parameters converge

Application: The art_dx diagnostic questions (Batch 2F) directly test each cell. Error patterns in mismatch cells reveal specificity-based reasoning and require different exp feedback than generic rule errors. Trenkic (2007) proposed an alternative "Syntactic Misanalysis" account (articles treated as adjectives, explaining omission with pre-modified NPs) — both accounts inform our NP complexity variation rule.

Master's Binary System (1990, TESOL Quarterly)

The most effective pedagogical framework for article instruction. Simplifies articles into two operations:

OperationQuestionArticleExample
Classification"What kind?"a (singular count) / ∅ (plural, mass)"She's a teacher" · "I like ∅ coffee"
Identification"Which one?"the"Pass me the report" (we both know which)

Application: This maps directly to the "Do we both know which one?" heuristic already proven effective in Artem's exercises. The exercise drill progression (Stage 1→2→3) follows Master's recommended sequence: binary distinction first, then zero article, then full discourse tracking. Master's 2002 comparative study testing four approaches found the binary system showed the most significant improvement.

Discourse Dependence (Brown 1983, Jonz 1990)

52–70% of cloze items involve cohesion; 32% of article blanks require information beyond sentence borders. Jarvis (2002) found articles are more likely to be dropped in topic-continuation positions — precisely where "the" is most needed.

Application: All new B2+ article questions use two-sentence discourse stems by default. Isolated single-sentence blanks are only acceptable for zero-article rules and fixed expressions. This is the single highest-impact design change from the research.

REALEC Corpus Findings (HSE Moscow)

From 6,054 Russian learner essays with 134,608 error annotations:

Error typeShareNote
Incorrect "the" (overuse/misuse)54%Dominant error type — overuse, not omission
Article omission37%Spikes with pre-modified NPs (adj + noun)
Incorrect "a"9%Surprisingly rare

Application: Our error taxonomy (Section 3) aligns with corpus data. Russian speakers at B2 don't primarily omit articles — they misselect between a/the. This confirms that gap questions with a/the distractors are the right primary format, not input questions (which test omission more than misselection). Derkach & Alexopoulou (2024) found Russian speakers achieve only 55% accuracy with mass nouns — confirming zero-article questions deserve dedicated coverage.

Russian L1 Scaffolds

Russian demonstratives can bridge to English articles — with explicit limits:

RussianMaps toLimitation
этот (this)the (definite, present)Works for ~60% of "the" contexts
тот (that)the (definite, anaphoric)Breaks for structural "the" (the UK, play the piano)

Application: Include этот/тот bridge in exp fields for B1 article questions only (~50 questions). At B2+, drop the bridge — it doesn't cover generic, structural, or conventional uses. Exp must explicitly mark where the analogy breaks.

Fossilisation Risk

Implication for quiz design

Articles cannot be treated as a "teach once, move on" topic. The spaced repetition system must keep resurfacing article questions at all levels. The most fossilisation-resistant uses — definite singular generics ("the whale is endangered"), articles in pre-modified NPs, and abstract noun articles — belong at C1 level and should not be introduced prematurely. B1→B2 transition is the critical window for intensive practice.
