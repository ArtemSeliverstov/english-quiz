Detailed Descriptions Sentence Transformation (Key Word) What: Rewrite a sentence using a given key word, keeping the meaning identical. Cambridge FCE/CAE Part 4 format. Why it works: Forces active manipulation of grammar structures. Tests whether the learner truly understands a rule (not just recognises it). Bridges the recognition-production gap. Best for: Conditionals, passive voice, reported speech, gerunds & infinitives, modal verbs. Difficulty control: B1 = 2–4 word gap. B2 = 3–5 word gap. C1 = 3–6 word gap with idioms/collocations. Error Correction What: A paragraph with deliberate errors. Find and fix them. Why it works: Develops proofreading instinct. Particularly effective for articles (Russian speakers insert/omit articles by feel — this forces conscious analysis). Also builds awareness of typical L1 transfer errors. Best for: Articles, prepositions, tenses, subject-verb agreement, word order. Difficulty control: Vary error density (1 per sentence = easy, 2–3 per sentence = hard) and error subtlety. Dictation / Cloze Dictation What: Claude gives a sentence or short paragraph. Player types it from memory after reading it once. Or: Claude gives a gapped version, player fills blanks from memory. Why it works: Forces attention to exact forms — articles, prepositions, verb endings. Encodes collocations and phrasal verbs as chunks rather than individual words. Research shows dictation is one of the most effective methods for encoding collocations. Best for: Collocations, phrasal verbs, articles, irregular verb forms, prepositions. Difficulty control: Sentence length, display time, number of blanks. Guided Free Writing What: Write 3–5 sentences using specific target structures. Claude checks and gives feedback. Why it works: The only exercise type that requires genuine production in context. Exposes whether the learner can actually use a structure, not just fill a blank. Also builds vocabulary naturally. Best for: All weak areas, especially for players with large recognition-production gaps (Artem: input, Anna: everything). Difficulty control: Constrain the topic, required structures, and sentence count. Conversation Practice What: Simulated dialogue on a topic. Claude plays a role, corrects errors mid-conversation, and provides feedback at the end. Why it works: Builds fluency and automaticity. Effective for collocations and everyday English because it mimics real usage. Low-friction — feels like chatting rather than studying. Best for: Collocations, everyday English, phrasal verbs, vocabulary. Especially good for Anna (engagement-friendly). Reverse Translation (RU→EN) What: Claude gives a Russian sentence. Player writes the English equivalent. Claude checks grammar, word choice, and naturalness. Why it works: Exposes L1 transfer errors directly. The learner must consciously produce articles, choose between confusable words (say/tell, make/do), and select correct prepositions — all areas where Russian speakers rely on (wrong) intuition. Best for: Articles, word choice, prepositions, collocations. Particularly powerful for Russian speakers because it directly confronts the L1→L2 transfer pathway. Difficulty control: Sentence complexity, vocabulary level, number of transfer-prone structures per sentence. Particle Sort (PV Matching) What: Claude presents 6 base verbs and 6 particles. The player matches each verb+particle pair to a given meaning. No sentence context — pure particle-meaning mapping. Why it works: Artem's PV errors concentrate on particle confusion within verb families (GET+off vs GET+through vs GET+over). This exercise isolates the particle-meaning association that's currently missing — the exact cognitive link that fails during input production. By removing sentence context, it forces direct verb-particle-meaning wiring. Best for: Phrasal verbs — specifically for players who recognise PVs in context (high MCQ) but can't produce them (low input). Artem's 41-point MCQ-to-input gap is the textbook use case. Difficulty control: Easy = particles from different families (mix GET/TURN/BRING). Hard = all particles from one family (GET+6 particles). Hardest = reverse direction (given the PV, state the meaning). Format example: Base verbs: GET, GET, GET, GET, GET, GET Particles: off, through, over, out of, across, into Match to meanings: A. recover from illness or disappointment B. exit a bus or train C. avoid an obligation D. communicate an idea successfully E. survive a difficult period F. become interested in a hobby Session protocol: Present all 6 at once. Player answers A–F. Feedback after all 6 — don't reveal answers one by one (forces the player to hold all distinctions in working memory simultaneously). Rotate through verb families weekly: Week 1 = GET, Week 2 = TURN, Week 3 = BRING/COME, Week 4 = SET/TAKE, then repeat. exercise field: "particle_sort". Russian Trap (Anti-Calque Translation) What: Claude gives 8 Russian sentences specifically designed to trigger L1 calque errors. The player translates to English. After each answer, Claude shows: (1) the likely calque the player would produce, (2) the correct English with the right PV, (3) why the PV works and the calque doesn't. Why it works: Artem's exercise errors show a recurring pattern: when the correct PV doesn't come to mind, he falls back to literal Russian-to-English translation (расслабиться → "relax myself" instead of wind down; объехать → "drive around" instead of cut through). Standard translation exercises don't target this specifically. Russian Trap exercises are reverse-engineered from actual calque errors to confront the L1→L2 pathway where PV retrieval fails. Best for: Phrasal verbs where Russian has a single-word equivalent that the learner reaches for instead of the English PV. Also effective for collocations (делать фото → "make photos" instead of take photos). Article Decision Drill What: Claude presents 2–3 sentence mini-scenarios with article blanks (___). The player fills in a/an/the/∅ for each blank. After each item, Claude provides corrective feedback naming the specific error pattern (a→the / zero / prep+art / fixed / dropped). Why it works: Articles are discourse-dependent — the choice between "a" and "the" depends on whether a referent was previously established. Isolated sentence blanks miss this (Brown 1983: 32–70% of article decisions require cross-sentence context). Two-sentence stems force the learner to track reference chains. Master's (1990) binary system ("classify or identify?") provides the decision framework. Uniquely effective for Russian speakers because L1 has no article system — this builds the decision-making habit from scratch. Best for: Articles — all error patterns. Especially a→the swap (Pattern 1, ~40% of errors), zero-article overuse (Pattern 2), preposition+article interactions (Pattern 3). Works for all family members. Best Practices & Research Why These Exercise Types Were Chosen The Recognition-Production Gap Language learning research consistently shows that learners can recognise correct forms (MCQ, gap-fill) long before they can produce them. The Grammar Quiz excels at testing recognition and recall. But all three players show a measurable gap: Artem's MCQ accuracy (84%) vs input accuracy (70%) is a 14-point spread. Anna's gap is even larger. The exercises in this guide are specifically designed to push learners across that gap. L1 Transfer Effects for Russian Speakers Russian lacks articles entirely, uses different preposition logic, and has flexible word order. These create persistent "fossilised" errors that don't respond well to more MCQ practice — the learner needs to produce the correct form repeatedly in context to overwrite the L1 default. Reverse translation (RU→EN) is especially effective because it forces the learner through the exact cognitive pathway where transfer errors occur. Spaced Repetition Meets Production The Grammar Quiz already implements spaced repetition for its question bank. Supplementary exercises should follow the same principle: if a learner struggles with phrasal verbs on Monday's transformation exercise, Wednesday's dictation should include those same phrasal verbs. The Firebase tracking makes this possible — Claude can check previous exercise results and adapt. Session Design Principles 10 minutes beats 60 minutes. Short, focused sessions produce better retention than long marathons. Especially true for Anna. Mix exercise types within a week, not within a session. Each session should focus on one exercise type + one grammar area for maximum depth. Production before recognition. Do the free writing / translation exercise BEFORE the quiz, not after. Producing errors first makes the quiz correction more impactful. Track everything, analyse weekly. The value of these exercises compounds when Artem can see trends: "Your article accuracy improved from 68% to 79% over 3 weeks of error correction practice." Russian explanations when needed. For Anna especially, Claude should explain grammar rules in Russian when the English explanation isn't landing. This isn't a crutch — it's efficient teaching. Error Correction Best Practices (for Claude) Correct errors inline during conversation practice, not in a big list at the end (which feels like a test result). Use "sandwich" feedback: praise → correction → encouragement. For repeated errors, don't just correct — explain the rule briefly and give a mnemonic if possible. Never correct pronunciation/spelling in free writing unless it changes meaning. Focus on grammar and naturalness. Claude must follow them when running article exercises. 1. Master's Binary System — the decision framework All article exercises use Peter Master's (1990) two-question framework as the core scaffold: QuestionAnswerArticle "Am I classifying — saying what kind of thing?"Yesa (singular count) / ∅ (plural, mass) "Am I identifying — saying which specific one?"Yesthe This maps to Artem's proven heuristic: "Do we both know which one?" → THE. Master's 2002 comparative study testing 4 approaches found this binary system showed the most significant improvement. 2. Discourse stems, not isolated sentences Brown (1983): 32–70% of article decisions require cross-sentence context. All article drills at Stage 2+ must use two-sentence stems. Sentence 1 establishes the referent; Sentence 2 has the blank. Single-sentence stems are only acceptable for zero-article rules and fixed expressions. 3. Russian bridge scaffolds at B1 only For Nicole, Ernest, and Anna, use this bridge in feedback: "Think: would you say этот/тот (this/that one) in Russian? If yes → English needs 'the'. If no → probably 'a' or ∅." ⚠ Caveat (must be stated): "This works for ~60% of 'the' contexts. It does NOT work for: play the piano, the UK, 'the whale is endangered'." At B2+ (Artem, Egor), drop the Russian bridge — use English metalanguage only. The bridge becomes misleading for generic, structural, and conventional uses of "the". 4. Start with convergence cells, then mismatch From the Fluctuation Hypothesis (Ionin 2004): Russian speakers get [+def,+spec] and [−def,−spec] mostly right (parameters converge). They fail on [+def,−spec] ("the manager on duty, whoever that is" → they use "a") and [−def,+spec] ("I'm looking for a particular report" → they use "the"). Start each drill with 2 convergence items, then introduce mismatch items. 5. Name the pattern, don't just correct After each item, feedback must state: (1) correct answer, (2) which of the 5 patterns it falls under, (3) the pattern-specific explanation. Generic "the answer is THE" feedback is forbidden — it doesn't build the decision-making habit. 6. Vary NP complexity REALEC corpus: article omission spikes with pre-modified NPs ("important role" → omits "an"). ~30% of drill items should use adjective + noun stems to test processing automaticity alongside rule knowledge.
